Citizens band radio     citizens band radio ( also known as cb radio ) is , in many countries , a system of short-distance radio communications between individuals on a selection of 40 channels within the 27 mhz ( 11 m ) band . citizens band not the same as the amateur radio service ( `` ham '' radio ) which has a much longer range . in many countries , cb operation does not require a license to operate . it may be used for business or personal communications . like many other two-way radio services , citizens band channels are shared by many users . only one station may transmit at a time . all other stations must listen and wait for the shared channel to be available . it is customary for stations waiting to use a shared channel to broadcast the single word `` break '' ( or `` breaker '' ) during a lull in the conversation . this lets other people using the channel know that others are waiting . in the us , channel 19 is most often used by truckers . channel 9 is reserved for emergencies . the citizens band radio service originated in the united states as one of several personal radio services regulated by the federal communications commission ( fcc ) . these services began in 1945 to permit citizens a radio band for personal communication . in 1948 , the original cb radios were designed for operation on the 460â€“470 mhz uhf band . there were two classes of cb radio : `` a '' and `` b '' . class b radios had simpler technical requirements , and were limited to a smaller frequency range . al gross established the citizens radio corporation during the late 1940s to manufacture class b handhelds for the general public . in the 1960s it became popular with taxicab companies to communicate with their drivers . in the 1970s it became popular with people and truckers using it in their vehicles . the popularity was sparked by cb radios and the jargon used in several movies . truckers in particular began using it to warn other truckers of police speedtraps .
